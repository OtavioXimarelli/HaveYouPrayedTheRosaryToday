## The Youngest of the Doctors

Marie-Françoise-Thérèse Martin was born on January 2, 1873, in Alençon, France. She was the youngest of nine children, five of whom survived — and all five sisters became Carmelite nuns.

She entered the Carmel of Lisieux at age 15, after a personal audience with Pope Leo XIII (she literally touched the Pope's knee to ask for permission — a bold gesture that scandalized those present and charmed the pontiff). She died of tuberculosis on September 30, 1897, at just 24 years old.

In 1997, Pope John Paul II declared her a **Doctor of the Church** — the youngest and one of only four women with that title. Her *Story of a Soul* became one of the most widely read spiritual books of the 20th century.

## The "Little Way"

Thérèse's central contribution to Christian spirituality is what she called *la petite voie* — the **Little Way** of spiritual childhood.

In contrast to spiritualities that demand extraordinary penances, long fasts, and heroic mortifications, Thérèse proposed:

> "The elevator that must lift me up to heaven are your arms, O Jesus! For this, I do not need to grow; on the contrary, I must remain small, become smaller and smaller."

This "little way" is not conformism or spiritual laziness — it is a **profound theological act**: recognizing our radical dependence on God and trusting that He makes up for what we lack.

## Thérèse and the Rosary: An Honest Struggle

Here lies the reason why Thérèse resonates especially with ordinary people: she was completely **honest about her difficulties in prayer**.

She wrote in her letters that she often **fell asleep during the Rosary**. Instead of giving in to scruples, she turned it into theology:

> "Sometimes I sleep during prayer... Well, mothers and nursemaids love children who sleep in their arms."

It is not an excuse for laziness — it is the perception that God loves His children even when they are asleep, and that prayer is not a performance before a demanding God.

At the same time, Thérèse took the Rosary seriously. She prayed it walking in the garden of the Carmel, using it as a meditation on the mysteries of the life of Christ — not as an obligation to be "dispatched", but as a **conversation with a Mother**.

## The Theology of "Small"

For Thérèse, the Rosary perfectly represented the Little Way:

- **It is accessible to everyone** — it does not require theological education
- **It seems small** — humble repetition of simple Hail Marys
- **It is actually immense** — because what matters is not the size of the act, but the love with which it is done

> "What pleases God is not so much the sublime as the humble heart and good will."

She preferred **a single Hail Mary prayed with love** to a whole Rosary prayed by mechanical habit.

## The Shower of Roses

On her deathbed, Thérèse promised: *"I will spend my heaven doing good on earth. I will let fall a shower of roses."*

The shower of roses — graces, healings, consolations attributed to her intercession — became the sign of her presence. When Pius X decreed her canonization in 1925, a contemporary observed: *"She did nothing extraordinary — except love extraordinarily."*

## To Reflect

- Have you ever felt guilty for falling asleep or getting distracted during the Rosary? How does Thérèse's perspective change that?
- The "little way" challenges the mentality of spiritual productivity — the idea that prayers "count" more if they are long or difficult. How do you feel about praying a single Rosary with love, instead of in a hurry?
- What "small thing" can you do with extraordinary love today?

## Prayer to Saint Thérèse

*Saint Thérèse of the Child Jesus,*  
*who discovered the beauty of smallness and the strength of simple love,*  
*teach us to pray the Rosary without perfectionism and without fear.*  
*May we fall asleep in your arms like beloved children,*  
*knowing that God completes what we lack.*  
*Let fall upon us your shower of roses.*
